On our final and seventh stop through time, we will be traveling to South America to see the unique mammals that evolved as a result of the Great American Interchange. The location where most of these creatures lie is in ancient Paraguay. Giant ground sloths, terror birds and large cats are some of the pre-Ice Age beasts before the mammoths came to dominate the land. Out at sea lies a massive monster shark and whales ranging from big to small. Though some of you might request seeing the Ice Ages after this time period, I'm afraid that this is as far as Time Masters Inc. will allow on the tour.
Strange huh? A mammal that has a unique appearance is sure to fascinate you. While we travel the grasslands, you will see herds of Macrauchenia run in the same way that herds of Zebra do today. These herbivores would often outrun predators such as Smilodon or Phorusrhacos, sometimes they would even use their back legs to deliver powerful kicks. You will also notice that Macrauchenia has a trunk. Despite that, it is not related to elephants.
Not an ordinary sloth, but a giant one. That's the best way to describe Megatherium in its entirety. Weighing more than modern elephants, these giant ground sloths would often use their huge claws to forage for food at heights unreachable to other herbivores. These claws were also used as weapons for defense against predators such as Smilodon. In case one comes in our direction, we'll move the tour vehicle so that it doesn't view us as a threat.
A predator that was both fast and aggressive towards its prey, the flightless Phorusrhacos has another name; Terror Bird. These birds would prey upon helpless young animals and would scavenge carcasses of full-grown animals. The Smilodon were considered their rivals on the plains of Paraguay and would often clash with them. Let's hope they don't chase anyone down while on the tour.
You'll know this predator when you witness it live. The sabre-tooth cat known as Smilodon is the mightiest creature to have ever lived in ancient South America. Though originally from North America, this cat arrived in South America when the two continents merged with each other. Like lions today, a single male would look over his pride and have cubs with the females. Some males often bear scars from their battles with rivals. Give a shout-out if you see a half-tooth male wandering the plains of Paraguay on the tour.
We know what you'll say out at sea with this monster; "You're gonna need a bigger boat." For those of you who have a fear of sharks, we advise you to remain calm at all times. Megalodon is the biggest carnivorous fish that ever lived and it lived up to this name by feasting on the large whales out in open water. Due to safety concerns, we have employed professional divers to use shark cages to get live underwater footage for the tour guests.
Before we head out to open water to find megalodon, we'll have to start the second half of the tour in shallow water. Young megalodon are common, but you'll also find other creatures such as the odobenocetops more friendly. Like narwhals, odobenocetops has sharp tusks which it used for mating rituals or to forage for food. They are startled easily, so be careful when you approach them for a visit.
