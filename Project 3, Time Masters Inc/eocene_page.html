On our sixth stop through time, we will take a stop in the mangrove environment of the Eocene period. As you will see, much time has passed since the dinosaurs walked the Earth. In their place, the mammals have emerged as the new age known as the Cenozoic is beginning to replace the Mesozoic era. The location that we will be stopping at is a mangrove that will one day become the Sahara Desert. In the trees above, primates leap from branch to branch. On the beach, large mammals forage for food. Out at sea, large whales are the top predators. The Dorudon image below was taken in the wild by our professional photographer.
A primitive monkey that jumped from tree to tree, Apidium was quite possibly one of the very first of its kind. Evolving from the nocturnal Godinotia, Apidium lived in mangrove environments where it foraged for fruit and stayed in the trees to avoid being eaten by predators below. As we traverse the mangroves, look above to see it leap in the same way that modern monkeys do today.
How about we talk about something bigger in the mangrove. It may look like a rhino, but the Arsinoitherium is directly related to elephants. Though it is a large mammal with an aggressive appearance, I think you should know that Arsinoitherium is a gentle giant. Mostly a swimmer, Arsinoitherium would sometimes come onto land in order to mate or find feeding grounds. Perhaps on the tour you could feed one a fruit.
This relative of the elephant is smaller than the Arsinoitherium, but I'm sure many of you will find it interesting as well. Moeritherium is semi-aquatic because it would often swim in rivers around the mangrove environments to find food in the riverbed. Moeritherium is not very fast on land or in the water, but I'm sure you will have plenty of time to observe them for yourself.
Farther out from the mangroves is a large predator that belongs to a family of creatures many of you would consider to be gentle, the whales. Basilosaurus was the first giant whale to roam the seas and was considered the top predator of its time. This species of whale didn't have a blow-hole and had to get air by raising its nose to the surface. Its diet consisted of fish, sharks, squid, turtles and other marine mammals such as Dorudon. To get a good look at this whale, we have created a speaker to draw any nearby for whale watching.
Basilosaurus is not the only whale in the Eocene period, there is also Dorudon. This species of whale is smaller and often travels in groups to protect their young. This is similar to how dolphins are today. Like Basilosaurus, Dorudon had no blow-hole. I should mention that this whale is very fast, so it has an advantage over the larger whales. On the tour, you will have to watch and see it jump out of the water for the thrill of the adventure.
