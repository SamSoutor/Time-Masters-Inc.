On our second stop through time, we will be traveling to the Devonian period. Due to complications with Time Masters Inc., I am not at liberty to discuss the location of where we will be stopping. I think you will find that this time period has more to it than the Ordovician did. On the land we have plants and amphibians, but in the water we have sharks and some terrifying fish. Due to the danger that some of these species possess, we have prepared an anti-shark cage so you can get close to the creatures. Other than that, welcome to The Age of Fish. Some of the images were taken in the wild by our professional photographer.
To those of you who didn't think that the Orthocones were scary, I think you will find the next sea monster to have your heart in your mouth. Dunkleosteus was considered to be the top predator of its time and you can see why. Part of an extinct family of fish known as placoderms, Dunkleosteus was a heavily armoured fish that showed no mercy to whatever it hunted. Instead of teeth, it had sharp bony plates which could cut through the toughest fish. We have tested chain mail on this monster with live bait and not even that is immune to its powerful bite. Those who have heart conditions or suffer from panic attacks should consult our medical advisor before viewing this magnificent fish.
We said that there were sharks in this time period, don't worry they won't harm you because these sharks are primitive. One such species is Stethacanthus. Often called the "Ironing Board" because of its strange fin, this shark used its fin for mating rituals and to frighten off potential predators. We can't risk open water dives with these creatures because there is a chance that a hungry Dunkleosteus could take you down.
Around river banks farther inland, you will find a predatory fish that can feast on all amphibians and sharks that cross paths with it. Called Hyneria, it is a large lobe-finned fish that has the ability to propel itself onto land using strong short fins. It also has an air bladder that acts as primitive lungs to survive in oxygen deprived waters. If we're lucky, we could see one catch a Hynerpeton while on the tour.
Not only are there fish in the Devonian, there are also amphibians. Say hello to Hynerpeton, one of the first vertebrate animals to live and move on the land. This amphibian had to make sure that its skin was wet because if the skin were to dry out, they would die. Fortunately at night, they could spend more time on land. Life was not easy for Hynerpeton because predators such as Stethacanthus and Hyneria would often hunt them down. Like most amphibians, the eggs of Hynerpeton had to be laid in the water. If you spot one on the tour, make sure to not startle them.
